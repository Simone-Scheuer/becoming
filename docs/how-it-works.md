# How It Works

## The Problem

You know what you should do. You don't do it. Not because you're lazy — because every morning is a blank slate of decisions, and the decision is where things break down. You make a plan, and by Wednesday it feels irrelevant. You set goals and forget you set them. You know the gym matters but somehow it never happens when work is exciting.

Becoming fixes this by removing the decision and replacing it with intelligence.

## Two Halves of One System

### The Architect

When you add a goal, the system doesn't just write it down. It interrogates it. "Is this feasible given your schedule? What value does this serve? What other goal does it compete with? What's the minimum viable version?" It models the relationship between your goals, checks them against your real available time, and tells you when you're overcommitting. You design your life. The system tells you if the design is realistic.

### The Infrastructure

Once the goals are set, the system handles everything in between. It reads your schedule, knows what time it is, and produces prescriptions fitted to your actual free blocks. It tracks every task, knows how long things have been sitting, and redistributes when reality diverges from the plan. You don't think about logistics. You think about doing the work.

## The Loop

Every day runs the same loop:

1. **Morning: the system prescribes.** It reads your goals, your schedule, your tasks, and what happened yesterday. It tells you what to do today — time-slotted, ordered by what matters most, matched to your energy patterns. You negotiate if you want. Then you go do it.

2. **Evening: you score, the system syncs.** What actually happened? The system updates everything — your weekly plan checkboxes, your task list, your goal progress counts. Tomorrow's morning check-in reads tonight's updates. Nothing falls through the cracks.

3. **Weekly: the system plans.** Every 7 days, it reviews the data — which goals are on track, which collapsed, what patterns emerged. It builds next week's plan with capacity modeling: "You have 22 free hours and 30 hours of work. Here's what fits."

## The Intelligence

The system builds models of how you work — not from assumptions, but from your data:

**It learns your energy.** After two weeks, it knows you do your best deep work in the morning and crash after lunch. It slots tasks accordingly.

**It learns your throughput.** You plan 10 things and do 7. The system adjusts: next week it plans 7 things into the same time, with buffer.

**It learns your avoidance.** That task has been sitting for 5 days. The system puts it in your peak energy block tomorrow — because avoidance needs the most cognitive resource, and that resource depletes by afternoon.

**It learns your recovery.** After a bad day, it reduces the prescription to minimums. It doesn't try to "make up" lost work. It gets you back to baseline.

**It catches your thoughts.** When you say "I don't belong in that class," the system names the pattern — "that's the comparison engine, not a fact" — redirects to your values and the specific task, and moves on. One sentence. Not therapy. Coaching.

## Why It Works (When Other Systems Don't)

**It prescribes, not asks.** "Here's what you should do today" not "what would you like to do?"

**It syncs.** When you say "I did the homework" tonight, tomorrow won't re-prescribe it. One source of truth.

**It reasons about time.** Prescriptions fit your real schedule. It won't tell you to do a 2-hour session when class starts in 45 minutes.

**It counts.** "3/4 sessions this week" is a fact. "Am I doing enough?" is a spiral.

**It has a floor.** Every goal has a bad-day version. The bar lowers without disappearing.

**It flags without guilt.** "Day 5. Do it or kill it." Facts, not shame.

**It protects what matters.** Music, play, creative noodling — things that die when tracked. The system leaves them alone.

**Goals have lifecycles.** They're born through dialogue, tracked weekly, and killed with dignity when they stop being real. A dead goal isn't failure — it's data.

## The Files

Everything is plain text. No database, no cloud, no vendor lock-in.

- `profile.md` — who you are, how you work, your intelligence models
- `values.md` — what matters to you (inferred, confirmed, refined)
- `schedule.md` — your weekly time grid
- `goals.md` — goals with lifecycle metadata
- `docket.json` — structured task store (system-managed)
- `patterns.md` — what the system has learned about you
- `weeks/` — weekly plans with daily checkboxes (the sync hub)
- `checkins/` — daily behavioral data
- `context/` — optional personal context for deeper coaching

## The Philosophy

Most people don't lack intelligence or desire. They lack a system that converts intentions into actions reliably, across days and weeks, through good moods and bad ones, without requiring the discipline they don't have.

Self-actualization isn't a trait you're born with. It's the symptom of a coherent system — values connected to goals connected to actions connected to feedback. When the system is coherent, you get out of your own way. When it's not, you spend all your energy on the mucky-gucky stuff in between wanting and doing.

This system was designed from inside that problem, not from outside it.
